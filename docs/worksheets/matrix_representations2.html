Matrix Representations

Given Bases

In ℝ³, consider the following bases:

\(S_1 = \left\{\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\right\}\) (the standard basis)

\(S_2 = \left\{\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} -3 \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\right\}\)

\(S_3 = \left\{\begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{3}/3 \\ \sqrt{3}/3 \\ \sqrt{3}/3 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{2}/2 \\ 0 \\ -\sqrt{2}/2 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} -\sqrt{6}/6 \\ \sqrt{6}/3 \\ -\sqrt{6}/6 \end{pmatrix}\right\}\)

In ℝ⁴, consider the following bases:

\(S_4 = \left\{\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\right\}\) (the standard basis)

\(S_5 = \left\{\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ -4 \\ -1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}\right\}\)

\(S_6 = \left\{\begin{pmatrix} 1/2 \\ 1/2 \\ 1/2 \\ 1/2 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 \\ 1/2 \\ -1/2 \\ -1/2 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 \\ -1/2 \\ 1/2 \\ -1/2 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 \\ -1/2 \\ -1/2 \\ 1/2 \end{pmatrix}\right\}\)

Part I: Bases and Coordinates

Let \(S = \{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n\}\) be a basis of ℝⁿ, and let \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n\). Then there exists a unique vector \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n\) such that \(x_1\mathbf{v}_1 + x_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + x_n\mathbf{v}_n = \mathbf{v}\). We denote this vector \(\mathbf{x}\) by \([\mathbf{v}]_S\), which represents the coordinates of \(\mathbf{v}\) with respect to the basis \(S\).

Problem 1

Find the vector \(\mathbf{v}\) given its coordinates in the specified basis:

  1. Given \([\mathbf{v}]_{S_2} = (1,0,-1)\)
  2. Given \([\mathbf{v}]_{S_3} = (0,-1,0)\)
  3. Given \([\mathbf{v}]_{S_5} = (1,0,-1,0)\)
  4. Given \([\mathbf{v}]_{S_5} = (1,1,1,1)\)

Problem 2

For each of the following vectors, find \([\mathbf{v}]_S\) by:

  1. Writing the vector equation \(\mathbf{v} = x_1\mathbf{v}_1 + x_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + x_k\mathbf{v}_k\)
  2. Converting it to a matrix equation
  3. Solving the augmented matrix using SymPy
  4. Determining \([\mathbf{v}]_S\)

Find coordinates for:

  1. The vector \(\mathbf{v} = (1,1,1)\) with respect to basis \(S_2\)
  2. The vector \(\mathbf{v} = (1,1,1)\) with respect to basis \(S_3\)
  3. The vector \(\mathbf{v} = (1,1,1,1)\) with respect to basis \(S_5\)
  4. The vector \(\mathbf{v} = (1,1,1,1)\) with respect to basis \(S_6\)
  5. Multiple vectors:
    1. With respect to \(S_2\), find coordinates of \(\mathbf{v}_1 = (1,1,0)\) and \(\mathbf{v}_2 = (0,1,1)\)
    2. With respect to \(S_6\), find coordinates of \(\mathbf{v}_1 = (1,1,0,-1)\), \(\mathbf{v}_2 = (0,1,1,-2)\), and \(\mathbf{v}_3 = (1,0,1,0)\)

Part II: Change of Basis Matrices

Let \(B_1 = \{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n\}\) and \(B_2 = \{\mathbf{w}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{w}_n\}\) be two bases of ℝⁿ. Then there exists a unique invertible \(n \times n\) matrix \(P = P_{B_1\leftarrow B_2}\) such that for every \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n\): \([\mathbf{v}]_{B_1} = P[\mathbf{v}]_{B_2}\).

The matrix \(P\) is the transition matrix from \(B_2\) to \(B_1\) and it can be shown that \(P = \begin{bmatrix} [\mathbf{w}_1]_{B_1} & [\mathbf{w}_2]_{B_1} & \cdots & [\mathbf{w}_n]_{B_1} \end{bmatrix}\)

Problems

  1. Find the transition matrix from \(S_2\) to \(S_1\)
  2. Find the transition matrix from \(S_1\) to \(S_3\)
  3. Find the transition matrix from \(S_2\) to \(S_3\)
  4. Suppose that \(S=\{\mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2,\mathbf{w}_3\}\) and that the \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) is the transition matrix from \(S\) to \(S_2\), find the vectors \(\mathbf{w}_1, \mathbf{w}_2, \mathbf{w}_3\).
  5. Suppose that \(S=\{\mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2,\mathbf{w}_3,\mathbf{w}_4\}\) and that \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix}\) is the transition matrix from \(S_5\) to \(S\), find the vectors \(\mathbf{w}_1, \mathbf{w}_2, \mathbf{w}_3, \mathbf{w}_4\)

Part III: Matrix Representation of Linear Maps

Let \(T: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n\) be a linear map and \(S = \{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n\}\) be a basis. The matrix representation of \(T\) with respect to \(S\) is the unique \(n\times n\) matrix that satisfies: \([T\mathbf{v}]_S = A[\mathbf{v}]_S\).

It can be shown that \(A = \begin{bmatrix} [T\mathbf{v}_1]_S & [T\mathbf{v}_2]_S & \cdots & [T\mathbf{v}_n]_S \end{bmatrix}\)

Problems

  1. Find the matrix representation of \(T\) with respect to the basis \(S=\{\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\mathbf{v}_3\}\) given: \(T\mathbf{v}_1 = -2\mathbf{v}_1\), \(T\mathbf{v}_2 = 8\mathbf{v}_2\), \(T\mathbf{v}_3 = \mathbf{v}_3\).

  2. Given the matrix representation \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 & 0 \\ 0 & 8 \end{bmatrix}\) with respect to a basis \(S=\{\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2\}\):

    1. Find \(T(\mathbf{v}_1)\) and \(T(\mathbf{v}_2)\)
    2. Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors

  3. For the linear transformation \(T(x,y,z) = (x+y, 2x-y+z, z-3x)\):

    1. Prove that \(T\) is linear
    2. Find the matrix representation with respect to basis \(S_1\)
    3. Find the matrix representation with respect to basis \(S_2\)

  4. Given the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) with respect to basis \(S_2\):

    1. Find \(T((1,1,-1))\)
    2. Find \(T((1,1,1))\)
    3. Find the vector \(\mathbf{v}\) such that \(T\mathbf{v} = (-3,-3,4)\)
    4. Find the matrix representation with respect to basis \(S_1\)

  5. Given the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\) with respect to basis \(S_6\):

    1. Find \(T((1/2,1/2,1/2,1/2))\)
    2. Find \(T((1,0,0,0))\)
    3. Find \(T((0,1,0,0))\)
    4. Find a basis for \(\text{Nul}(A)\) and a non-zero vector \(\mathbf{v}\) such that \(T\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\)

Part IV: Matrix Representations with Respect to Two Bases

Let \(B_1\) and \(B_2\) be bases of ℝⁿ and let \(T: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n\) be a linear map with matrix representations:

\[[T\mathbf{v}]_{B_1} = A[\mathbf{v}]_{B_1}\quad\text{and}\quad[T\mathbf{v}]_{B_2} = B[\mathbf{v}]_{B_2}\]

If \(P = P_{B_1\leftarrow B_2}\) is the transition matrix, then: \(P^{-1}AP = B\) and \(A = PBP^{-1}\)

Problems

  1. Given a diagonal matrix representation \(\text{diag}(-1,2,0,4)\) with respect to basis \(S_6\):
    1. Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors
    2. Find the matrix representation of \(T\) with respect to \(S_6\), and call this matrix \(A\).
    3. Find the matrix representation of \(T\) with respect to \(S_4\), and call this matrix \(B\).
  2. Find \(P=P_{S_6\leftarrow S_4}\), the transition matrix from \(S_4\) to \(S_6\), and verify that \(P^{-1}AP = B\).

Part V: Finding Diagonal Representations

For a linear map \(T: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n\) with basis \(S = \{\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_n\}\), a diagonal representation has the form:

\[[T\mathbf{v}]_S = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix}[\mathbf{v}]_S.\]

The vectors \(\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_n\) are eigenvectors with corresponding aigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n\).

The equivalent statement for matrix is similar. Suppose that the \(n\times n\) matrix \(A\) has linearly independent eigenvectors \(\mathbf{v}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{v}_n\) with corresponding eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n\). Let \(P=[\mathbf{v}_1 \cdots \mathbf{v}_n]\) and \(\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n)\). We can check that \(P\) is invertible, that \(AP = P\Lambda\), or equivalently, that \(P^{-1}AP=\Lambda\).

Problems

  1. Given \(A = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 12 \\ -1 & 5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 4 \\ -1 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 4 \\ 1 & -3 \end{bmatrix}\):

    • Read off the eigenvalues and eigenvectors, verify that \(A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v}\), and find \(P\) and \(P^{-1}\)

  2. For the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 4 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix}\):

    1. Find the eigenvalues without computation
    2. Find the eigenvectors, \(P\), and \(\Lambda\) (you can use software for this part)

  3. For the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} -3 & -5 & -2 \\ -5 & 0 & -5 \\ -2 & -5 & -3 \end{bmatrix}\):

    1. Use SymPy to find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors and verify that \(A=P\Lambda P^{-1}\).
    2. Do the same using NumPy, but since the matrix is symmetric use eigh instead of eig.

  4. Repeat Problem 3 for the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 2 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\)

  5. For the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}\):

    • Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors, and determine if \(A\) is diagonalizable

  6. Repeat Problem 5 for the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ -2 & -1 & 2 \\ -2 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)
